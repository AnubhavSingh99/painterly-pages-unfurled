# **Character Lore – Aarvi**

### **“The girl who ended up in the wrong world, but kept drawing the one she truly belonged to.”**

Aarvi is a quiet, deeply introverted girl who was never really meant for the life she was pushed into.
On paper, she is an engineering student — someone who attends classes, submits assignments, and blends into the background. But in truth, that world was never hers.

Her real world has always been made of **sketchbooks, ink stains, unfinished character concepts, music in her earphones, and dreams too big for the room she grew up in**.

She is the kind of girl who speaks little, notices everything, and feels more than she can explain.
Most people think she is shy, distant, or “too quiet,” but inside her mind lives an entire universe — full of character designs, storylines, visual worlds, movement, color, and emotions she cannot say out loud.

Art is not just a hobby for her.
It is her escape, her language, and the only place where she feels fully real.

---

## **Core Backstory**

Aarvi grew up in an environment where she often felt restricted — emotionally, mentally, and creatively.
Home never felt like a space where she could fully become herself. Because of that, she learned to disappear quietly into her own world.

That world slowly became her refuge:

* she drew in silence,
* imagined stories behind strangers,
* created fictional people more honest than the real ones around her,
* and dreamed of one day living far away, somewhere she could finally breathe.

Even though life pushed her into **engineering college**, her heart never left art.

She sits in classrooms solving things she never asked to solve, while her notebook margins fill with:

* character faces,
* outfit ideas,
* comic panels,
* imagined cityscapes,
* and scenes from a future she desperately wants.

She dreams of leaving everything behind someday and moving abroad — especially to **New York City** — where she imagines a tiny but beautiful studio apartment with large windows, a messy art desk, dim lights, music playing, and her own freedom.

There, she wants to live not as someone’s daughter, student, or “safe choice” —
but as **herself**:
an artist,
a dancer,
a creator,
and someone who builds worlds for others to feel seen in.
